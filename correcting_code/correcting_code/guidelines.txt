For a PhD thesis on heuristic search applied to Neural Architecture Search (NAS), the strongest evaluation is usually not just "did it find a good architecture?" but:

Solution quality
Search efficiency
Generalization
Ablation of the heuristic
Statistical significance

A useful framework is to treat your heuristic as a search algorithm and evaluate it the same way heuristic search researchers evaluate planners or optimization methods.

1. Compare Against Strong Baselines

7. Statistical Evaluation

NAS is highly stochastic.

Run:

20–50 seeds if feasible
At minimum 10 seeds

Report:

μ±σ

for:

best accuracy
search time
architectures evaluated

Use:

Wilcoxon signed-rank test
Mann–Whitney U
paired t-test (if assumptions hold)

Report p-values and effect sizes.

8. Scalability Analysis

Show what happens as search space grows.

For example:

Search Space Size	Random	Your Method
10^3	200 evals	40 evals
10^4	2000 evals	120 evals
10^5	20000 evals	400 evals

This demonstrates why the heuristic matters.

10. If Your Thesis Is Primarily About Heuristic Search

Then your main research questions should look like:

RQ1: Does the heuristic reduce search effort?

RQ2: Does it improve anytime performance?

RQ3: Does it scale better than uninformed search?

RQ4: Does it maintain solution quality while reducing computational cost?

RQ5: How sensitive is performance to heuristic accuracy?

Those questions are often more scientifically interesting than a small improvement in final test accuracy.

A strong PhD-level evaluation typically includes:

NAS-Bench experiments (controlled search evaluation)
Real training experiments (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet subset)
Regret/anytime curves
Search-cost analysis
Heuristic ablations
Statistical significance tests

That combination demonstrates both search-theoretic merit and practical NAS effectiveness, which is exactly what examiners usually look for in a heuristic-search thesis.



Expected Research Claims

After this evaluation, you should be able to make claims such as:

The heuristic reduces node expansions by 70–90%.
The heuristic reaches near-optimal architectures 5–10× faster.
Search cost scales sublinearly compared with baseline methods.
Architecture quality is maintained or improved.
Improvements are statistically significant (p < 0.05).
Heuristic estimates strongly correlate with true architecture performance.

Those six claims together form a very strong heuristic-search-for-NAS thesis evaluation.
